DaVinci Resolve: Interactive Color Panel Guide

Click on the tabs below to explore each color tool from your screenshots.

1. Primaries - Color Wheels

What Is It?

This is the **Primaries Color Wheels** panel, your main and most fundamental tool. It's used for "primary" color correction, which means making broad adjustments to the entire image to balance it and set the overall look.

When & Why Do We Use It?

You use this panel **first** on almost every shot.

**Why?** To correct problems and set a "look".
1. **Correction:** Fix exposure (too dark/bright) and white balance (too blue/orange).
2. **Grading:** Create a mood (e.g., a cold, blue look for a winter scene or a warm, saturated look for a sunset).

What Do the Tools Do?

  • Lift (Shadows): Controls the dark areas. Drag the center puck to tint the shadows (e.g., add blue). Drag the wheel underneath to make shadows brighter or darker.
  • Gamma (Midtones): Controls the middle-gray areas. This is where skin tones and most details live. It has the biggest impact on the image's perceived brightness.
  • Gain (Highlights): Controls the bright areas. This adjusts the "whites" of your image.
  • Offset:** Adjusts the *entire image at once* (shadows, midtones, and highlights). Great for a quick, large exposure fix before fine-tuning.
  • Temp & Tint:** Your main white balance controls. **Temp** makes the image warmer (orange) or cooler (blue). **Tint** corrects any green or magenta shift.
  • Contrast & Pivot:** **Contrast** increases/decreases the difference between light and dark. **Pivot** sets the center point for the contrast adjustment.
  • Saturation:** Increases or decreases the color intensity for the whole image.
  • Color Boost:** A "smart" saturation that mainly boosts less-saturated colors, protecting skin tones from looking unnatural.

How Do We Use It? (A Basic Workflow)

  1. Step 1: Balance:** On your first node, use `Temp` and `Tint` to get a correct white balance (e.g., make a white wall look white).
  2. Step 2: Set Exposure:** Use `Lift` to set your "black point" (make the darkest part just above pure black). Use `Gain` to set your "white point" (make the brightest part just below pure white).
  3. Step 3: Adjust Midtones:** Use `Gamma` to set the main brightness of the shot. This is your primary skin tone adjustment.
  4. Step 4: Set Saturation:** Use the `Saturation` slider to add or remove the desired amount of color. A value of 50 is default.

2. HDR - High Dynamic Range Wheels

What Is It?

This is the **High Dynamic Range (HDR) Wheels** panel. Think of it as a super-precise version of the Primaries wheels. It breaks the image into many specific "zones" (Dark, Shadow, Light, etc.) and lets you adjust them individually.

When & Why Do We Use It?

You use this when the Primaries wheels are too "blunt" and affect parts of the image you don't want to change.

**Why?** For precision control.
**Example:** You want to make the bright sky darker, but when you use the `Gain` wheel, it also darkens the actor's face. With HDR Wheels, you can adjust the `Light` zone (the sky) without touching the `Shadow` zone (where the face might be).

What Do the Tools Do?

  • Zone Wheels (Dark, Shadow, Light, Global): Each wheel controls a specific brightness range. By default, you see four, but you can enable more.
  • Zone Graph (Top): The small graph at the top shows you the *range* of each zone. You can drag these to change what "Shadow" vs. "Light" means, giving you total control.
  • Exp (Exposure): The master wheel under each zone. This controls the brightness of *only* that zone.
  • Sat (Saturation): The slider under each zone. This controls the saturation of *only* that zone.
  • Global:** A wheel that acts like the `Offset` wheel, affecting the entire image.

How Do We Use It? (Example)

  1. Goal:** Darken a bright sky without affecting the actor.
  2. Step 1:** Select the `Light` wheel (this zone usually contains the sky).
  3. Step 2:** Drag the `Exp` (Exposure) master wheel underneath it downwards. You will see the sky get darker, while the rest of the image is mostly untouched.
  4. Step 3:** If the effect is too strong or not strong enough on the sky, drag the zone controls in the graph at the top to fine-tune exactly what brightness range the `Light` wheel is affecting.

3. Curves - Custom

What Is It?

This is the **Custom Curves** panel. It's a classic, powerful tool that gives you visual control over the image's contrast and color. The graph represents your image's tonal range, from pure black (bottom-left) to pure white (top-right).

When & Why Do We Use It?

You use this for fine-tuning contrast and creating stylized color tints.

**Why?** It offers more granular control than the simple `Contrast` slider.
1. **For Contrast:** Creating a gentle "S-Curve" is the most common way to add cinematic contrast.
2. **For Color:** You can manipulate the individual Red, Green, and Blue curves to add color tints to specific tonal ranges (e.g., add blue to shadows, yellow to highlights).

What Do the Tools Do?

  • The Graph/Line:** The diagonal line is your image. Clicking to add a point and dragging it **up** makes that part of the image brighter. Dragging **down** makes it darker.
  • Y, R, G, B Buttons:** By default, you are on `Y` (Luminance), which controls all three channels together for brightness/contrast. You can click `R`, `G`, or `B` to control *only* that color channel.
  • Soft Clip:** A tool to "roll off" or compress the highlights/shadows to prevent them from clipping (losing detail) and to create a softer, more filmic look.

How Do We Use It? (Creating an S-Curve)

  1. Step 1:** Ensure the `Y` button is selected.
  2. Step 2:** Click on the line in the top-right quadrant (highlights) and drag it gently **up** to brighten the highlights.
  3. Step 3:** Click on the line in the bottom-left quadrant (shadows) and drag it gently **down** to darken the shadows.
  4. Result:** You have created an "S" shape, which adds contrast to your image in a smooth, pleasing way.

4. ColorSlice

What Is It?

This is the **ColorSlice** panel, a modern "vector" tool. It's a **secondary grading** tool, meaning it's used to adjust *specific colors* after your primary balance is done. It breaks the image into six "slices" (Red, Skin, Yellow, Green, Cyan, Blue, Magenta) and lets you adjust them independently.

When & Why Do We Use It?

You use this for targeted "secondary" adjustments.

**Why?** It's incredibly fast and intuitive for common tasks.
**Examples:**
- "The actor's `Skin` tone is too red, I want it more orange."
- "I want the `Blue` sky to be darker and more saturated."
- "I want to change the `Green` grass to look more yellow and autumnal."

What Do the Tools Do?

  • Color Slices (Red, Skin, etc.):** Each vertical column controls *only* its named color.
  • Color Wheel:** Lets you change the **Hue** of that slice. (e.g., in the `Green` slice, push the wheel towards yellow).
  • Center:** A slider to fine-tune the center-point of the hue you're affecting.
  • Vertical Sliders:** The two main vertical sliders control the **Density** (brightness) and **Saturation** (color intensity) of *only that slice*.

How Do We Use It? (Enhancing a Sky)

  1. Goal:** Make a blue sky "pop".
  2. Step 1:** Go to the `Blue` slice.
  3. Step 2:** Drag the right-hand vertical slider (Saturation) **up** to increase the blue's intensity.
  4. Step 3:** Drag the left-hand vertical slider (Density) **down** to make the blue sky richer and darker.
  5. Result:** The sky is now enhanced, while the rest of the image (skin tones, grass, etc.) is untouched.

5. RGB Mixer

What Is It?

This is the **RGB Mixer**. It's a highly technical tool that gives you direct control over the R (Red), G (Green), and B (Blue) color channels that make up your image. It lets you "mix" how much of each original channel contributes to the final output channels.

When & Why Do We Use It?

You use this for creating highly stylized "film looks" or for solving complex color-contamination problems.

**Why?**
1. **Stylized Looks:** You can create "unreal" colors (e.g., making green foliage look yellow or red) to emulate certain film stocks.
2. **Monochrome:** It's fantastic for black & white conversion, giving you control over how bright each original color becomes in the B&W image.

What Do the Tools Do?

  • Output Columns (Red, Green, Blue):** Each column controls what "goes into" that final channel.
  • Sliders (R, G, B):** The sliders under the **Red Output** column, for example, control "How much of the *original* Red, Green, and Blue will make up the *new* Red channel?"
  • Preserve Luminance:** A crucial checkbox. When ticked, Resolve tries to maintain the image's original brightness, even as you drastically shift colors. This lets you change hues without making them look darker or brighter.
  • Monochrome:** Ticks this to create a B&W image. The sliders now control the B&W "mix".

How Do We Use It? (Autumn Look)

  1. Goal:** Make green foliage look more yellow/orange.
  2. Step 1:** Tick the `Preserve Luminance` box.
  3. Step 2:** Go to the `Green Output` column.
  4. Step 3:** Drag the `G` (Green) slider **down** (e.g., to 0.50). This removes green from the green channel.
  5. Step 4:** Drag the `R` (Red) slider **up** (e.g., to 0.50). This adds red *into* the green channel.
  6. Result:** The green areas of your image will shift towards yellow/orange, but their brightness will be preserved.

6. Color Match

What Is It?

This is the **Color Match** panel. It's an *automatic* color correction tool that uses a physical color chart (like the "ColorChecker" shown) to instantly balance your shot.

When & Why Do We Use It?

You use this at the **very beginning** of your grade, on footage where you filmed a color chart on set.

**Why?**
1. **For a Neutral Start:** It gives you a 100% technically accurate, neutral image in one click.
2. **To Match Different Cameras:** This is its best use. If you have a shot from a Sony and a shot from a Canon, they'll look different. If you film the chart with both and "Color Match" both clips, they will be perfectly matched to the same standard, making them easy to grade together.

What Do the Tools Do?

  • Chart Selector:** A dropdown (top right) where you tell Resolve *which* chart you used (e.g., "Calibrite ColorChecker Classic").
  • Source Gamma, Target Gamma, Target Color Space:** You tell Resolve the source (often "Auto") and the target you want to convert to (usually "Rec.709").
  • Match Button:** The button you press to make the magic happen.

How Do We Use It? (A Basic Match)

  1. Step 1:** In your viewer, change the dropdown from "Qualifier" (eyedropper) to "Color Chart".
  2. Step 2:** A grid will appear. Drag this grid over the color chart in your video footage and align the squares.
  3. Step 3:** In the `Color Match` panel, select the chart you used (e.g., "ColorChecker Classic").
  4. Step 4:** Set your `Target Gamma` and `Target Color Space` (usually Rec.709 for both).
  5. Step 5:** Click `Match`. Resolve will instantly analyze the colors and apply a correction.

7. Camera Raw

What Is It?

This is the **Camera Raw** panel. This panel *only* works if your source footage is a **RAW video format** (like Blackmagic RAW, REDCODE, ARRI RAW, etc.). It allows you to "develop" the raw sensor data *before* it even enters the color grading pipeline.

When & Why Do We Use It?

This is the **absolute first step** in your workflow, done before you even add a color grading node.

**Why?** To get the best possible image quality from your file. Changing "Exposure" here is *not* the same as using the `Gain` wheel; it's like changing the ISO or exposure *in the camera*. You can recover huge amounts of "lost" detail from highlights and shadows that would be impossible to fix on a normal video file.

What Do the Tools Do?

  • Decode Quality:** Lets you work at a lower resolution (e.g., "Half res.") to get better playback performance while editing. You can set it back to "Full res." for the final export.
  • Decode Using:** The most important setting. Change this from "Camera metadata" to **"Clip"**.
  • "Clip" Controls:** Once "Decode Using" is set to "Clip", this panel unlocks controls for **ISO**, **Exposure**, **Color Temp**, **Tint**, **Highlight Recovery**, and more, specific to that camera's RAW format.

How Do We Use It? (Recovering a Shot)

  1. Step 1:** Select your RAW clip.
  2. Step 2:** Go to the `Camera Raw` panel.
  3. Step 3:** Change `Decode Using` to **"Clip"**.
  4. Step 4:** Adjust the `Exposure`, `ISO`, and `Color Temp` sliders to get a good, neutral-looking base image. If your highlights are blown out, try ticking the "Highlight Recovery" box (if available).
  5. Step 5:** Now, go to your nodes and begin your *creative* grade using the Primaries wheels.

8. Motion Effects

What Is It?

This is the **Motion Effects** panel. This tool doesn't affect color. Instead, it deals with **Noise Reduction (NR)** (removing grain) and adding **Motion Blur**.

When & Why Do We Use It?

You use this to clean up footage or fix motion issues.

**Why?**
1. **Noise Reduction:** To clean up "noisy" or "grainy" footage that was shot in low light. Resolve's noise reduction is one of the best available.
2. **Add Motion Blur:** To fix "stuttery" footage shot at a high shutter speed, or to make visual effects and titles look like they "belong" in the scene.

Warning: Noise Reduction is *extremely* intensive on your computer's GPU and will slow down playback.

What Do the Tools Do?

  • Temporal NR:** The most powerful NR. It reduces noise by comparing pixels *across multiple frames* (over time). It's great at removing "dancing" noise.
  • Spatial NR:** Reduces noise by comparing pixels *within the same frame*. It's more like a smart blur.
  • Motion Blur:** *Adds* artificial motion blur to your clip.

How Do We Use It? (Basic Noise Reduction)

  1. Step 1:** Add a new node (often the very last node in your tree).
  2. Step 2:** Go to the `Temporal NR` section.
  3. Step 3:** Set `Frames` to 1 or 2.
  4. Step 4:** In the `Temporal Threshold` box, *gently* increase the `Luma` (brightness noise) and `Chroma` (color noise) sliders.
  5. Step 5:** Play the clip and watch for artifacts. Use the *lowest* possible value that cleans up the noise, as high values can create "ghosting" or make the image look waxy.
